How does this estimate work?

Important: This calculator only works with a U.S. AQI value for PM2.5, also called fine-particle pollution. An overall AQI value may instead be based on ozone, PM10, carbon monoxide, sulfur dioxide, or nitrogen dioxide. If PM2.5 is not the pollutant responsible for the AQI value, this conversion does not apply.

Step 1: The calculator estimates the 24-hour average PM2.5 concentration represented by the entered AQI. It uses the EPA's current PM2.5 AQI breakpoints and linear interpolation to convert the AQI value into a concentration measured in micrograms per cubic meter (μg/m³).

This reverse conversion is an estimate, not an exact reconstruction of the original measurement. When the EPA calculates an AQI, PM2.5 concentrations are truncated to one decimal place and the resulting AQI is rounded to the nearest integer. Some precision has therefore already been removed from the reported AQI.

Step 2: Berkeley Earth developed a rough comparison based on estimated health effects. Under that comparison, an average PM2.5 concentration of 22 μg/m³ for one day is approximately equivalent to the health impact of smoking one cigarette.

The calculator divides the estimated PM2.5 concentration by 22, then multiplies it by the fraction of a day entered:

cigarette equivalent = (PM2.5 concentration ÷ 22) × (hours exposed ÷ 24)

This calculation assumes that the entered PM2.5 level remains constant throughout the selected exposure period. Real air quality can change significantly from hour to hour.

Step 3: The result is rounded to the nearest whole cigarette. Because of this, an estimated equivalent below half a cigarette will display as zero. The PM2.5 concentration shown above the explanation provides a more detailed indication of the calculated exposure.

What does the result mean?

This number is a rough way to communicate the possible health impact of PM2.5 pollution. It does not mean that breathing polluted air is chemically identical to smoking cigarettes, and it is not a measure of the exact amount of smoke or particles inhaled.

Actual exposure and health effects can vary based on the source and composition of the pollution, whether a person is indoors or outdoors, physical activity, breathing rate, age, existing health conditions, and other factors. This calculator is informational and should not be treated as medical advice or a personal risk assessment.

Sources: EPA AirNow AQI Calculator and Berkeley Earth's Air Pollution and Cigarette Equivalence .
